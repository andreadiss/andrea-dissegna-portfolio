Qualitative project: usability of a food delivery app & sentiment analysis

+

Context

The start up has developed the prototype of a platform similar to Skyscanner but for food delivery, which allows users to find the best offers (prices, delivery times, etc.).

Goal

Identify the missing or problematic functionalities that influence the user experience of their website.

Secondary Research

61% of the users love the convenience
59% of users love the digital payment option
40% of the users love the flexibility
65% of Gen Z users love that it's cheaper

User Types

Young Urban Professional

Age: 25â€“35 years

Characteristics: Lives in the city, has a career with intense working hours.

Needs: Seeks options for quick and practical meals, prefers to avoid cooking after a workday.

Habits: Uses the app to order food, often during dinner, trying different cuisines.

Values: Convenience, variety of choices, ability to personalize orders.

Gen Z Student

Age: 18â€“24 years

Characteristics: University student or young adult, often on a limited budget.

Needs: Desires fast and economical meals, ideal for a dynamic social life or studying.

Habits: Uses the app for shared meals with friends or quick meals, attracted by promotions, discounts, and programs.

Values: Affordable prices, simplicity in ordering, ability to pay digitally.

Material Preparation

This is an example of prompt review to create a bias-free discussion guide for the interview.

Process
  1. Initial Prompt:

    Context: Imagine you are a UX researcher. You must interview two user groups: young urban professionals (25â€“35 years) and Gen Z students (18â€“24 years). Task: Create a list of 10 open-ended questions, focusing on the use of a food delivery app that is clear and neutral, suitable for both groups. Tone: Maintain a neutral and professional tone.

  2. Initial Output (low quality):

    "How often do you use food delivery apps, and what are your main needs or expectations?"

    This output question combines two distinct inquiries, making it harder for respondents to provide focused answers and for researchers to analyze responses effectively.

  3. Prompt Revision:

    Generate a list of 10 single open-ended questions [...]

    Questions will:

    • Explore use context, motivations, expectations, and pain points.
    • Investigate the frequency of use of food delivery apps.
    • Identify factors influencing users' choice of the food delivery app to use.
    • Allow the interviewer to notice differences between the two users profiles.
  4. Revised Output:
    • "How often do you use food delivery apps?"
    • "In what situations do you use food delivery apps?"
    • "What are the main reasons you choose a particular app over others?"

Methodology

  • In-depth interviews
  • Moderated usability test

Analysis

  • Affinity diagram
  • Sentiment analysis (per journey map)

I created the journey map by analyzing users' interviews with natural language models. Below example of neutral, negative, and positive comments of real users as classified by the algorithm.

Tools: Python + Hugging Face (NLP) + Inkscape

The journey map obtained from the sentiment analysis

"I'm looking for the login, which is usually top right, and there it is."

Insights

The login button opened a new page, and participants had to press the 'Back' button to return to the main page and continue their order. Users expect the 'Back' button to return them to the previous page in the history without retaining or using newly entered data. This discrepancy causes confusion.

Actionables

Simplifying the transition between login and the main page: Implement a login pop-up that does not replace the entire page but instead overlays the underlying content. Ensure the pop-up displays only the essential fields for login, simplifying the interface while keeping the location selection bar visible on the main screen.

Mixed-method project: improving Virtual Reality experience in cruise prototyping

+

Context

A company specializing in the development of cruise ship interior prototypes aims to utilize virtual reality to improve the identification of design errors compared to traditional 2D drawings and static 3D models. The objective is to leverage virtual reality to enhance error detection during the prototype phase, ensuring a more efficient and accurate design process. However, so far, they have not observed any significant advantages of VR over traditional models.

Goal

Improve design errors detection during technical checks by enabling active exploration in immersive virtual environments.

Methodology

  • Contextual enquiry
  • A/B testing

VR Immersion in a ship prototype

Analysis

Contextual Enquiry

Field observations during the use of virtual reality in a real working environment.

Interviews with technicians to assess their habits and identify barriers to VR adoption (e.g., health concerns that may affect compliance).

Insights

The technicians tended to remain static while using virtual reality, interacting with it similarly to traditional 2D or 3D rendering methods. Consequently, the core advantage of virtual realityâ€”immersion through active interactionâ€”was not fully exploited, resulting in no meaningful improvement over conventional rendering techniques.

Actionables

We encouraged technicians to actively engage with the virtual environment, instructing them to behave as if they were physically present aboard the ship, interacting dynamically with their surroundings. We subsequently evaluated the effectiveness of this intervention.

A/B Test

Analysis with a logistic model (binomial data error detected / error missed).

Results show that VR can significantly enhance the detection of design errors, reducing the number of misreported errors.

After our intervention, VR proved to be more efficient, with fewer design errors going unnoticed.

Quantitative project: increasing the understanding of diagnostic test results

+

Context

During the pandemic, the Public Health Minister asked us to assess the public's understanding of the effectiveness of swabs in order to inform communication strategies.

Goal

Identify critical areas in the publicâ€™s understanding of test results for SARS-CoV-2 and their behavioral implications.

Secondary Research

Few data on who the users of food delivery app are:

72% of users are under 44 years old
61% of the users love the convenience
59% of users love the digital payment option
40% of the users love the flexibility
65% of Gen Z users love that it's cheaper

User Types

Targeted general public, aged 18+ years, including Italians and English speakers across various nations.

Methodology

  • Questionnaires
  • Multivariate tests

Recruitment Online:

  • Age > 18 years
  • Italians and English speakers
  • 500+ questionnaires in various nations
Tools: Online programming of the questionnaire using Qualtrics and recruiting with Prolific.

Item Types:

  • Multiple choice and Likert scale questions
  • Sliding bars
  • Classification tasks
  • Inclusion of attention controls

Analysis

  • Creation of R script to automate database management and statistical analysis
  • Linear and multinomial models, frequency analysis, and association tests
  • Inclusion of covariates: age, gender, education, perceived risk

Insights

People tend to overestimate false positives (the probability that a test returns a positive result for a non-infected person) and underestimate false negatives (the probability that a test returns a negative result for an infected person). This can lead to excessive trust in negative results and inappropriate behaviors (e.g., abandoning precautionary measures).

Actionables

Targeted communication interventions should clarify the importance of repeating the test after a negative result, especially in high-prevalence contexts.
